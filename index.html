Storyboard → full AI video, in minutes

Instead of generating clips shot-by-shot, you build one storyboard image and let a video model turn the whole sequence into footage. Here is the workflow, condensed.

Source — “How to Turn a Storyboard into a Full AI Video in Minutes,” Jack Vs. AI (May 2026) · youtube.com/watch?v=Is4wgEpPMJQ

02:18

The missing stepLock your references first

Most people skip straight to generating shots, then fight to keep characters, style, and locations consistent across clips. The fix is to settle those before any video exists.

  1. Build a style reference sheet.A single image that defines the look — color grade, lighting mood, lens feel, overall aesthetic you want every shot to share.
  2. Build a location reference sheet.Pin down the setting so the environment stays the same from panel to panel.
  3. Build a character reference sheet.Define any recurring subjects so they don’t drift across shots.Go to Character reference sheet prompt maker →

Why it matters → these sheets are what you’ll feed into the storyboard step to enforce consistency for free.

Generate the planMake a storyboard with GPT Image 2

The storyboard is a single image laid out as a grid of panels — each panel is one shot of your sequence. This one image becomes the blueprint for the entire video.

  1. Engineer the prompt with Claude.Describe the sequence shot by shot — framing, camera angle, action, and mood for each panel — so the storyboard reads as a coherent set of shots rather than random frames.
  2. Generate the storyboard in GPT Image 2.Run the engineered prompt to produce the multi-panel board. (Nano Banana Pro is the alternative image model mentioned for the same job.)
  3. Carry the references through.Use your style and location sheets so every panel stays on-model. Regenerate until the panels are consistent and read clearly as a sequence.

The point → you’re making one strong, consistent image — not ten separate ones — which is what makes the next step fast.

Generate the footageAnimate it with Seedance 2.0

Rather than generating each shot on its own, you hand the whole storyboard to the video model and let it produce the sequence in one pass.

  1. Feed the storyboard image into Seedance 2.0.The model reads the panels and turns them into a moving sequence — no shot-by-shot generation, no manual stitching of separate clips.
  2. Review the output sequence.Check that motion, pacing, and shot order match what the panels implied.
  3. Iterate where needed.If a beat misses, adjust the storyboard or prompt and regenerate, rather than patching individual clips.

Best for → quickly prototyping ideas, short films, and testing product ads.

16:17

The honest takeWhere this shines, where it doesn’t

Strengths

  • Extremely fast — full sequence from one image instead of clip-by-clip work.
  • Consistency comes built in when you start from reference sheets and a single board.
  • Ideal for prototyping concepts, short films, and ad tests.

Trade-offs

  • Less granular control over any single shot than generating them individually.
  • Output quality leans heavily on how clean and consistent the storyboard is.
  • Better suited to fast iteration than to locked, final-production footage.
